AIR QUALITY STANDARDS AND HEALTH GUIDELINES

WHO AIR QUALITY GUIDELINES (2021 UPDATE)

PARTICULATE MATTER (PM2.5)
Fine particulate matter with diameter ≤2.5 micrometers penetrates deep into lungs and bloodstream.
- WHO Annual guideline: 5 µg/m³ (revised down from 10)
- WHO 24-hour guideline: 15 µg/m³ (3-4 exceedance days/year acceptable)
- US EPA Annual Standard: 9 µg/m³ (2024 revision)
- US EPA 24-hour Standard: 35 µg/m³

Health effects by concentration:
- 0-12 µg/m³: Good - little or no risk
- 12-35 µg/m³: Moderate - unusually sensitive may be affected
- 35-55 µg/m³: Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups - heart/lung disease, elderly, children
- 55-150 µg/m³: Unhealthy - everyone may experience effects
- 150-250 µg/m³: Very Unhealthy - serious effects for everyone
- 250+ µg/m³: Hazardous - emergency conditions

PARTICULATE MATTER (PM10)
Coarse particles with diameter ≤10 micrometers
- WHO Annual guideline: 15 µg/m³
- WHO 24-hour guideline: 45 µg/m³
- Causes respiratory irritation, aggravates asthma

OZONE (O3) - Ground Level
Formed when pollutants from cars, power plants react with sunlight.
- WHO 8-hour guideline: 60 µg/m³ (100 ppb)
- US EPA 8-hour Standard: 70 ppb
- Health effects: chest pain, coughing, throat irritation, reduced lung function
- Especially harmful for children, elderly, people with asthma

NITROGEN DIOXIDE (NO2)
From combustion processes (vehicles, power plants, industry)
- WHO Annual guideline: 10 µg/m³ (revised from 40)
- WHO 24-hour guideline: 25 µg/m³
- Health effects: Irritates airways, aggravates respiratory diseases

SULFUR DIOXIDE (SO2)
From fossil fuel combustion and volcanic activity
- WHO 24-hour guideline: 40 µg/m³
- WHO 10-minute guideline: 500 µg/m³
- Health effects: Respiratory irritation, bronchoconstriction

CARBON MONOXIDE (CO)
From incomplete combustion
- WHO 1-hour guideline: 35 mg/m³
- WHO 8-hour guideline: 10 mg/m³
- Health effects: Reduces oxygen delivery to organs, headaches, dizziness, death at high levels

AIR QUALITY INDEX (AQI) - US EPA SCALE
0-50: GOOD (Green)
- Air quality is satisfactory, air pollution poses little or no risk
- Who is at risk: No one

51-100: MODERATE (Yellow)
- Air quality is acceptable; however, for some pollutants there may be a moderate health concern for a very small number of people
- Who is at risk: Unusually sensitive people

101-150: UNHEALTHY FOR SENSITIVE GROUPS (Orange)
- Members of sensitive groups may experience health effects
- General public not likely to be affected
- Who is at risk: People with heart or lung disease, older adults, children, people with diabetes

151-200: UNHEALTHY (Red)
- Everyone may begin to experience health effects
- Members of sensitive groups may experience more serious health effects
- Who is at risk: Everyone

201-300: VERY UNHEALTHY (Purple)
- Health alert: everyone may experience more serious health effects
- Who is at risk: Everyone

301+: HAZARDOUS (Maroon)
- Health warnings of emergency conditions
- Entire population more likely to be affected
- Who is at risk: Everyone

WILDFIRE SMOKE IMPACTS
During wildfire events, AQI can spike dramatically:
- PM2.5 can reach 500+ µg/m³ in severe fires
- Smoke contains CO, volatile organic compounds, heavy metals
- Visibility reduced to less than 1 mile at PM2.5 > 250 µg/m³
- Even healthy individuals should limit outdoor exposure when AQI > 150

VULNERABLE POPULATIONS FOR AIR QUALITY
1. People with asthma or COPD - most sensitive to PM2.5 and ozone
2. Cardiovascular disease patients - PM2.5 triggers heart attacks
3. Children under 18 - lungs still developing
4. Adults over 65 - less efficient at defending against pollution
5. Outdoor workers - higher exposure duration
6. Pregnant women - linked to low birth weight, preterm birth
7. People in poverty - less ability to reduce exposure (no AC, outdoor jobs)

PROTECTIVE ACTIONS BY AQI LEVEL
Good (0-50): No action needed. Good day for outdoor activities.

Moderate (51-100): Sensitive groups should consider reducing prolonged outdoor exertion.

Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups (101-150):
- Sensitive groups should avoid prolonged outdoor exertion
- Move activities indoors or reschedule
- Wear N95 mask if outdoor activity necessary

Unhealthy (151-200):
- Everyone should reduce outdoor activity
- Keep windows closed, use air purifiers
- Wear N95 mask outdoors
- Check on elderly neighbors

Very Unhealthy (201-300):
- Avoid all outdoor physical activity
- Stay indoors with air purification
- Use N95 masks if must go outside
- Emergency plans for vulnerable people

Hazardous (301+):
- Remain indoors
- Seal doors and windows
- Consider evacuation if sustained
- N95/P100 respirators required outdoors
- Contact health authorities

INDOOR AIR QUALITY
Indoor pollution often 2-5x worse than outdoor:
- Cooking smoke: PM2.5 spikes
- Radon: #1 cause of lung cancer in non-smokers
- Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs) from paints, cleaning products
- Carbon monoxide from appliances
- Mold spores: trigger asthma and allergies

Solutions:
- Ventilation: Open windows when outdoor air is clean
- HEPA air purifiers: Filter PM2.5 effectively
- CO detectors: Required in sleeping areas
- Radon testing: Mitigation if above 4 pCi/L

GLOBAL AIR POLLUTION STATISTICS
- 7 million premature deaths per year from air pollution (WHO)
- 99% of world population breathes air exceeding WHO limits
- PM2.5 is the 5th leading risk factor for death globally
- Cost of air pollution: $5 trillion/year in welfare losses
- South and East Asia bear the highest burden

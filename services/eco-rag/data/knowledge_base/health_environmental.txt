ENVIRONMENTAL HEALTH - HUMAN IMPACTS AND CLINICAL GUIDANCE

POLLUTION AND HUMAN HEALTH

RESPIRATORY DISEASES FROM AIR POLLUTION
Asthma:
- 300 million people worldwide affected
- PM2.5 and ozone are strongest triggers
- Urban children have 40% higher asthma rates near highways
- Emergency room visits increase by 10% per 10 µg/m³ PM2.5 increase
- Management: Avoid triggers, use prescribed medications, have written action plan

COPD (Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease):
- 380 million affected globally
- 70% of cases attributable to tobacco + air pollution
- Long-term PM2.5 exposure accelerates lung function decline
- Cannot be reversed - prevention is critical

Lung Cancer:
- Outdoor air pollution classified as Group 1 carcinogen by IARC
- PM2.5 increases lung cancer risk even in non-smokers
- Diesel exhaust particularly carcinogenic

CARDIOVASCULAR EFFECTS
Short-term exposure effects (hours to days):
- Elevated blood pressure
- Increased heart rate variability (loss of regulatory function)
- Increased risk of heart attack and stroke
- PM2.5 triggers inflammation and blood clotting

Long-term exposure effects (years):
- Atherosclerosis (artery hardening)
- Hypertension
- Reduced heart function
- 15-20% increased cardiovascular mortality per 10 µg/m³ PM2.5

NEUROLOGICAL EFFECTS
- PM2.5 can cross blood-brain barrier
- Linked to cognitive decline and dementia
- Prenatal exposure linked to autism spectrum disorder
- NO2 exposure linked to childhood cognitive impairment
- Depression and anxiety higher in polluted areas

REPRODUCTIVE HEALTH
- PM2.5: Linked to low birth weight, preterm birth
- Lead exposure: Developmental delays, miscarriage
- Endocrine disruptors (from plastics, pesticides): Affect fertility

CLIMATE-RELATED HEALTH THREATS

HEAT-RELATED ILLNESS PREVENTION
Prevention strategies:
- Stay hydrated: 8+ glasses of water/day during heat events
- Avoid alcohol and caffeine (dehydrating)
- Wear loose, light-colored clothing
- Use sunscreen (SPF 30+) for outdoor activities
- Acclimatize gradually (takes 7-14 days in heat)
- Cool down: Cold water immersion most effective for heat stroke

Warning signs requiring immediate medical attention:
- Body temperature >103°F (39.4°C)
- Hot, red, dry or damp skin
- Rapid, strong pulse
- Confusion, slurred speech
- Loss of consciousness

VECTOR-BORNE DISEASES AND CLIMATE CHANGE
Malaria:
- 247 million cases in 2021, 619,000 deaths
- Climate change expanding malaria-suitable regions to higher altitudes and latitudes
- Prevention: Insecticide-treated bed nets, indoor residual spraying, antimalarial prophylaxis
- Treatment: Artemisinin-based combination therapies (ACTs)

Dengue Fever:
- 400 million infections/year, rising rapidly
- Aedes aegypti mosquito range expanding with warming
- No specific treatment - fluid management critical
- Prevention: Eliminate standing water, mosquito repellents, protective clothing
- Outbreak conditions: Temperature 20-35°C + rainfall + urban crowding

Lyme Disease:
- Blacklegged tick (Ixodes) range expanding northward in North America
- Symptoms: Bull's-eye rash, fever, fatigue, joint pain
- Prevention: Tick checks, long clothing in wooded areas, DEET repellents
- Treatment: Antibiotics effective if caught early

WATERBORNE DISEASES FROM CLIMATE IMPACTS
Cholera:
- Climate change increasing extreme rainfall + flooding = higher risk
- Vibrio cholerae thrives in warm coastal waters
- Can kill within hours if untreated
- Treatment: Oral rehydration solution (ORS) - saves 99% of cases
- Prevention: Clean water, sanitation, vaccination

Cyanotoxins from algal blooms:
- Warm, nutrient-rich water triggers cyanobacteria blooms
- Microcystin: Liver toxin, skin irritation
- Cylindrospermopsin: Kidney and liver damage
- Symptoms: Nausea, vomiting, rash, liver damage
- Do NOT swim in water with visible algal bloom or algal scum

MENTAL HEALTH AND CLIMATE
Eco-anxiety:
- Chronic fear of environmental catastrophe
- More prevalent in young people (18-34 age group)
- 68% of Americans report eco-anxiety (APA 2020)
- Management: Action-taking, community connection, professional support

Climate grief and solastalgia:
- Grief for lost environments and ecosystems
- "Solastalgia" = distress caused by environmental change in one's home
- Higher rates in communities experiencing direct climate impacts
- Support: Community resilience programs, mental health services

PTSD from climate disasters:
- Hurricane survivors: 30-50% show PTSD symptoms
- Wildfire evacuees: Elevated rates of depression and anxiety
- Flood victims: Long-term mental health impacts (especially children)
- Support: Access to mental health services, community support programs

NUTRITION AND FOOD SECURITY
Climate impacts on nutrition:
- CO2 fertilization: Reduces protein, zinc, iron in staple crops
- Extreme heat: Reduces crop yields, increases toxin-producing fungi
- Drought: Reduces diversity of available foods
- Flooding: Crop losses, food price spikes

Malnutrition and climate:
- 800 million people chronically hungry
- Climate change threatens progress on food security
- Children most vulnerable to nutrition impacts
- Stunting linked to poor nutrition affects cognitive development

ENVIRONMENTAL JUSTICE AND HEALTH EQUITY
Disproportionate impacts:
- Low-income communities: Located near highways, industrial facilities, waste sites
- Communities of color: Face 56% more PM2.5 from inequitable source placement
- Indigenous communities: Loss of traditional food sources, cultural connections to land
- Global South: Least responsible for climate change, most affected

Health burden disparities:
- Poor urban communities: 3x more asthma hospitalizations
- Environmental racism: 1 in 6 climate-related deaths in BIPOC communities preventable
- Occupational exposure: Farm workers, construction workers bear highest pollution burden

Solutions - Environmental Justice:
- Community air monitoring networks
- Participatory environmental planning
- Green infrastructure in underserved communities
- Health impact assessments for new developments
- Cumulative impact screening tools

EMERGENCY MEDICAL PROTOCOLS - ENVIRONMENTAL

Smoke Inhalation:
- Move to fresh air immediately
- Loosen clothing, monitor breathing
- Call 911 if: wheezing, chest tightness, confusion, loss of consciousness
- Do NOT give water to someone who is unconscious
- Supplemental oxygen is primary treatment

Chemical Exposure (Eyes):
- Flush with clean water for 15-20 minutes
- Remove contact lenses if present and safe to do so
- Call Poison Control: 1-800-222-1222 (US)
- Do NOT rub eyes

Pesticide Poisoning:
- Remove from exposure area
- Call Poison Control immediately
- Do NOT induce vomiting unless instructed
- Bring product container to hospital

Radiation Emergency:
- Remove outer clothing (removes 80% of contamination)
- Wash body with soap and water thoroughly
- Go to designated decontamination site
- Follow instructions from radiation protection authorities

HEALTHCARE SYSTEM PREPAREDNESS FOR CLIMATE EVENTS
Hospital surge capacity planning:
- Pre-positioning medications and supplies for climate events
- Backup power systems (hospitals cannot lose power)
- Water storage for extended operations
- Communication systems independent of commercial networks
- Mutual aid agreements between health facilities
- Telemedicine for communities cut off during disasters

Community health resilience:
- Community Health Workers as first-line responders
- Training in basic disaster health response
- Medication management for chronic disease during disasters
- Mental health first aid training for community members

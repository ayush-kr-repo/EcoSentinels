OCEAN SYSTEMS, SEA LEVEL RISE, AND COASTAL COMMUNITIES

OCEAN WARMING AND ACIDIFICATION

Current State:
- Oceans have absorbed >90% of excess heat from greenhouse gas emissions
- Sea surface temperatures at record highs: 2023 was hottest ocean year on record
- Marine heat waves: 54% of ocean surface experienced heat wave conditions in 2023
- Ocean heat content (0-2000m depth) continues to break records annually

Ocean Acidification:
- Pre-industrial pH: 8.2 → Current pH: 8.1 → 2100 projection: 7.95 (high emissions)
- Each 0.1 pH unit decrease = 26% increase in acidity (logarithmic scale)
- Calcium carbonate dissolution: Shells and skeletons of marine life dissolve
- Pteropods ("sea butterflies"): Already showing shell dissolution in Southern Ocean
- Coral calcification: 20% reduction at current acidification levels
- Economic impact on fisheries: $1 billion/year in US shellfish industry at risk

CORAL REEF SYSTEMS

Coral Bleaching:
- Occurs when water temperature exceeds normal by >1°C for 4+ weeks
- Symbiotic algae (zooxanthellae) expelled → coral turns white → coral starvation
- 4°C temperature anomaly = coral mortality within weeks
- Mass bleaching events:
  - 1998: First global bleaching event, 16% of world's coral died
  - 2016: Great Barrier Reef lost 50% of shallow water coral
  - 2024: 4th global bleaching event — most extensive on record

Great Barrier Reef:
- World's largest coral ecosystem: 2,300km, 900 islands
- Supports 1,500 fish species, 4,000 mollusk species
- $6.4 billion/year to Australian economy
- Lost 50%+ of coral since 1985
- 2016 and 2017: Consecutive bleaching events affected 91% of surveyed reefs
- At 2°C warming: Annual bleaching would prevent reef recovery

Reef Restoration:
- Coral gardening: Growing coral fragments on underwater nurseries
- Assisted evolution: Selectively breeding heat-tolerant coral
- Coral micro-fragmentation: Splitting coral to accelerate growth
- Cold water pumping: Cooling reef water during heat events (experimental)
- Shade structures (Reef Rescue): Fabric to reduce solar radiation

SEA LEVEL RISE

Causes and Contributions:
1. Thermal expansion: Warmer water takes up more volume (~40% of rise)
2. Mountain glacier melt (~20% of rise)
3. Greenland Ice Sheet melt (~20% of rise and accelerating)
4. Antarctic Ice Sheet melt (~10-15% of rise, high uncertainty)

Current Rate and Projections:
- 1900-1970: 1.5mm/year average rise
- 1970-2010: 2.0mm/year average rise
- 2010-2020: 4.3mm/year average rise (accelerating)
- 2050 (likely range): 20-30cm above 2000 baseline
- 2100 (low emissions): 30-60cm
- 2100 (high emissions): 60-100cm (some estimates to 2m with ice sheet instability)

Regional Variations:
- Sea level rise is not uniform globally
- US East Coast: Rising 3-4x faster than global average (AMOC slowdown)
- Alaska and Canada: Some land is rising (glacial rebound), partially offsetting
- Pacific Islands: Amplified by land subsidence from groundwater extraction
- Bangladesh: Compounding from sinking delta + sea level rise

Coastal Flooding:
- 100-year flood events becoming 10-year events in many locations
- Storm surge amplified by higher baseline sea level
- Nuisance flooding (sunny day flooding at high tides) increasing
- Miami: 400% increase in sunny day flooding since 2000
- Jakarta (Indonesia): Sinking 25cm/year + sea level rise = extreme vulnerability

VULNERABLE COASTAL COMMUNITIES

Small Island Developing States (SIDS):
- Maldives: 80% of land <1m above sea level — existential threat
- Tuvalu: First nation negotiating "migration with dignity" to New Zealand
- Kiribati: Already purchased land in Fiji for potential relocation
- Marshall Islands: National Emergency declared for climate adaptation
- 10% of world's population lives <10m above sea level

Mega-Deltas at Risk:
- Ganges-Brahmaputra (Bangladesh): 50+ million people at risk
- Mekong (Vietnam): Rice bowl of Asia under threat
- Nile Delta (Egypt): 30% of Egypt's agricultural land at risk
- Mississippi Delta: Losing land area equivalent to a football field per hour
- Yangtze Delta: Shanghai and 50 million people in high flood risk zone

Indigenous Coastal Communities:
- Shishmaref, Alaska: Voted to relocate entire village due to erosion
- Newtok, Alaska: Undergoing managed retreat — first US climate refugee community
- Louisiana Isle de Jean Charles: 98% land loss since 1955
- Traditional fishing communities losing access to ancestral waters

COASTAL ADAPTATION STRATEGIES

Hard Infrastructure:
- Sea walls and levees: Protect in short term; can worsen erosion elsewhere
- Storm surge barriers: Thames Barrier (London), MOSE (Venice)
- Beach nourishment: Sand pumping to replenish eroded beaches (temporary)
- Breakwaters: Reduce wave energy reaching shore

Nature-Based Solutions:
- Mangrove restoration: Mangroves reduce wave height by 50-70%, store carbon
- Coastal wetland restoration: Marshes absorb storm surge energy
- Oyster reef restoration: Breakwater effect + habitat + water quality
- Living shorelines: Hybrid approaches using natural materials
- Coral reef restoration: Reefs dissipate 97% of wave energy

Managed Retreat:
- Moving communities away from high-risk coastal areas
- Often the most cost-effective long-term strategy
- Faces social resistance due to community ties, property values
- Insurance markets increasingly driving retreat decisions
- Need supportive policies and funding for equitable implementation

EcoSentinels Coastal Monitoring:
- Integrates satellite sea level data from NASA/CNES SWOT mission
- Storm surge prediction linked to community alert system
- Coastal flood risk mapping by community
- Mangrove and seagrass health monitoring via remote sensing
- Supports coastal community early warning and evacuation planning

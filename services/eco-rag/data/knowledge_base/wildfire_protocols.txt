WILDFIRE SCIENCE, BEHAVIOR, AND COMMUNITY RESPONSE

WILDFIRE BEHAVIOR FUNDAMENTALS

Fire Triangle:
- Fuel: Vegetation (grass, shrubs, trees), structures, combustible materials
- Heat: Lightning, human activity, embers from existing fires
- Oxygen: Wind accelerates fire dramatically

Fire Spread Factors:
1. Wind: Primary driver of fire spread. Doubles speed at 12 mph; quadruples at 24 mph
2. Slope: Fire spreads twice as fast uphill for every 10-degree increase in slope
3. Fuel moisture: Below 10% = extreme fire danger; 30%+ = minimal fire spread
4. Fuel type: Fine fuels (grass) ignite easily; heavy fuels (logs) burn longer
5. Temperature: Higher temps = lower fuel moisture = faster ignition

Fire Weather Conditions:
- Red Flag Warning: Relative humidity <15%, wind speed >25mph, critically low fuel moisture
- Fire Weather Watch: Conditions likely within 24-48 hours
- Haines Index: Measures potential for large fire growth (scale 2-6; 6 = extreme)
- FFDI (Fire Danger Rating): Australian system (Low to Catastrophic)

WILDFIRE AND AIR QUALITY

Smoke Composition:
- PM2.5: Dominant pollutant in wildfire smoke; most dangerous to health
- Carbon Monoxide: Can reach dangerous levels near active fires
- Volatile Organic Compounds (VOCs): Hundreds of species in smoke
- Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons (PAHs): Carcinogenic compounds
- Ozone: Formed downwind from smoke plumes interacting with sunlight

Smoke Dispersion Factors:
- Wind direction: Determines which communities receive smoke impact
- Atmospheric stability: Inversions trap smoke near ground level
- Terrain: Mountains and valleys channel smoke
- Distance: PM2.5 can travel thousands of miles from source fires

Historical Wildfire Events:
- 2018 Camp Fire (Paradise, CA): 85 deaths, AQI >500 in Sacramento (200+ miles away)
- 2019-2020 Black Summer Australia: Burned 18.6 million hectares, smoke affected New Zealand
- 2021 Dixie Fire (CA): Largest single fire in CA history at 963,000+ acres
- 2023 Canada Wildfires: Smoke reached Europe; NYC recorded worst air quality in decades

COMMUNITY PREPAREDNESS

DEFENSIBLE SPACE (Home Ignition Zone)
Zone 1 (0-30 feet from home):
- Remove all dead plants, grass, weeds
- Clear debris from roof, gutters, decks
- Keep lawns green and trimmed to 4 inches
- Space trees 30+ feet apart
- Remove tree branches within 10 feet of ground
- No wood piles or propane tanks adjacent to house

Zone 2 (30-100 feet from home):
- Reduce flammable vegetation — thin trees, remove ladder fuels
- Space shrubs in clusters, 15+ feet apart
- Remove dead plant material
- Mow annual grasses to 4 inches

Home Hardening:
- Ember-resistant vents: Replace standard vents with 1/16" mesh
- Fire-resistant roofing: Class A materials (metal, tile, asphalt shingles)
- Multi-pane windows: Tempered glass or dual-pane
- Enclosed eaves: Exposed rafters are ignition points
- Non-combustible decking: Concrete, tile, or composite

EVACUATION PLANNING
Go-Bag (72 hours minimum):
- Water: 1 gallon per person per day
- Food: Non-perishable, no cooking required
- Medications: Full supply + prescriptions
- Documents: ID, insurance, medical records (in waterproof bag or USB)
- Phone + charger + portable battery
- Cash (small bills)
- N95 respirators (smoke protection)
- Change of clothes, sturdy shoes
- Pet supplies and carrier
- First aid kit

Family Communication Plan:
- Two meeting places: One near home, one outside neighborhood
- Out-of-state contact (easier to reach in local disaster)
- Know multiple evacuation routes
- Pre-plan what to take (can't think clearly in emergency)
- Register special needs with county emergency management

COMMUNITY ALERT SYSTEMS
- WEA (Wireless Emergency Alerts): Automatic alerts to mobile phones
- Nixle: Local government notification service
- Ready.gov: Federal emergency management resources
- Local emergency management: Know your county's system
- NOAA Weather Radio: Battery-powered radio for power outages

WILDFIRE RISK ASSESSMENT TOOLS
- USFS Fire Danger Rating: Daily assessment by forest type
- Copernicus Emergency Management Service: Satellite damage assessment
- EFFIS (European Forest Fire Information System): EU-wide monitoring
- NIFC (National Interagency Fire Center): US fire statistics
- Cal Fire: California-specific fire mapping and alerts

INDIGENOUS FIRE MANAGEMENT
Traditional Ecological Knowledge:
- Indigenous peoples have managed landscapes with fire for thousands of years
- Cultural burning: Low-intensity fires to manage vegetation, promote diversity
- Aboriginal fire management in Australia reduced catastrophic fire intensity
- Native American burning maintained prairie ecosystems and habitat
- Benefits: Reduces fuel accumulation, improves habitat diversity, maintains cultural landscapes

Modern Integration:
- Cultural burning being reintegrated into land management in US, Australia, Canada
- Evidence shows traditional burning reduces catastrophic fire risk
- Provides ecological benefits: biodiversity, soil health, wildlife habitat
- Being recognized in environmental policy frameworks

WILDFIRE AND CLIMATE CHANGE
Trends:
- Fire season in Western US has extended by 78 days since 1970
- Area burned annually in Western US has doubled since 1984
- Fire intensity increasing with heat and drought
- Large fires (>10,000 acres) now 7x more common than 1970s

Projections:
- 2°C warming: Annual burned area could increase 100-200% in Mediterranean regions
- Western US: Fire weather season extends 2-3 months by 2050
- Tropics: New fire-prone regions emerging
- Feedback loop: Fire emissions add CO2, accelerating climate change
